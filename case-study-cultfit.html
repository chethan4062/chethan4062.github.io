01 — Brand Overview

Not a gym. A fitness operating system.

Cult.fit doesn't sell products — it sells access. The performance marketing playbook here is fundamentally different from e-commerce D2C. The goal isn't a checkout; it's a first class booking that leads to a subscription that leads to a habit.

The Cult Pass Model — Why It Matters for Marketing
The Cult Pass is the single most important product decision for performance marketing. By offering unlimited classes at any center for one monthly price, it eliminates the biggest subscription objections: 'what if I travel?', 'what if I want to try a different class?', 'what if my centre is far?'. Every ad can promise unlimited access — and the promise is true. This makes conversion copy simpler, trust easier to build, and churn structurally lower.

The number that changes the entire CAC calculation: A ₹500 free trial that converts to a ₹2,000/month subscription with an 8-month average retention period = ₹16,000 LTV. This means Cult.fit can rationally spend 3–5× more to acquire a user than most e-commerce brands, because the payback window is longer and the revenue is recurring. Every performance marketing decision — bid strategy, target CPA, creative spend — must be anchored to this LTV math, not immediate conversion value.

What most gym chains miss — and Cult.fit understood — is that 80% of users who churned said they wanted to come back, but had scheduling conflicts, not dissatisfaction. This single insight rewrites the retargeting strategy entirely. You're not trying to convince lapsed members that Cult is good. They already know it's good. You're removing the friction that stopped them from renewing. That's a completely different message, creative, and offer.

The addition of Cult.sport (D2C fitness equipment and athleisure) is also a smart performance marketing play — it gives the brand an e-commerce acquisition channel that feeds the subscription funnel. Someone who buys a yoga mat from Cult.sport is a warm lead for a Cult Pass. The two products share infrastructure but serve as separate top-of-funnel entry points.

02 — Ad Strategy

Subscription marketing isn't e-commerce marketing.

The channels look similar — Meta, Google, YouTube — but the strategy, metrics, and creative are fundamentally different when you're selling a recurring experience, not a one-time product.

03 — Funnel Breakdown

The subscription funnel is not a purchase funnel.

The biggest mistake brands make when marketing subscriptions is treating it like e-commerce. The stages are different, the metrics are different, and the moments that matter most are different.

Top of Funnel
Awareness — Local Discovery & Aspiration
+
  • Google Search — local intent: 'Best gym in Bangalore', 'fitness classes near me', 'yoga classes Koramangala', 'HIIT near Indiranagar'. These are the highest-value queries Cult.fit can capture — the user is actively searching for exactly what Cult offers. Appearing in the top 3 local pack results here is more valuable than any brand campaign.
  • Instagram Reels — aspiration and FOMO: High-energy class clips showing real trainers, real members, real sweat. The creative doesn't sell Cult.fit — it shows you what you're missing. A 15-second clip of a packed HIIT class with pumping music creates FOMO that a static image or copy never could.
  • YouTube ads — class experience previews: Aspirational fitness content showing the full class experience — the warm-up, the intensity, the cool-down, the community. These 30-second cuts make you feel what a class feels like before you've ever walked in.
  • Word of mouth — the underrated channel: With a 90% recommendation rate, every satisfied member is an acquisition channel. Cult.fit's organic referral rate is likely their lowest-CAC acquisition path, driven by genuine product satisfaction. This isn't something you can buy — it has to be earned through product quality and community experience.
  • Google My Business — local trust signals: 300+ GMB profiles, each with class photos, current hours, specific service listings, recent reviews, and Q&A. When someone searches 'yoga classes near me', a GMB profile with 4.6★ from 800 reviews and current class schedule information converts at a fundamentally higher rate than a profile that hasn't been updated in 6 months.
Mid Funnel — Unique to Subscriptions
Trial & First Experience — The Product Demo
+
  • Free trial class as the conversion event: Meta lead gen ads and Google search ads driving to a 'Book Your Free First Class' landing page — optimized per city, per center, per class type. The CTA is a class booking, not a product purchase. This removes all financial risk and collapses the consideration stage dramatically.
  • Frictionless app onboarding: App download → location access → nearby centers → class schedule → one-tap booking. Every extra step in this flow is a drop-off point. The booking UX IS part of the product, and it's part of the performance marketing funnel — a clunky booking flow kills conversion rates that no ad creative can rescue.
  • First-class experience IS the ad: Trainer quality, class energy, facility cleanliness, member friendliness — these are the product demo. No ad creative can convert as effectively as a first class that exceeds expectations. Performance marketing's job is to get someone through the door; the product's job is to close the sale. The two have to work together.
  • First-week habit nudges: Welcome sequence push notifications guiding users to book their 2nd and 3rd classes within the first 7 days. Data shows that users who attend 3+ classes in their first week have dramatically higher subscription conversion rates. These nudges are free to send but have outsized impact on LTV.
  • Gamification from day one: 'You're on a 2-class streak! Book tomorrow to keep it going.' Streak mechanics and class completion badges create early habit loops that make subscription conversion feel like a natural next step rather than a purchase decision.
Bottom of Funnel
Subscription Conversion — Locking In the Habit
+
  • In-app post-trial prompts: After the free trial class, in-app messaging: 'Lock in your Cult Pass — ₹1,999/month for unlimited classes at any center'. The ask comes at the peak of engagement, immediately after the best possible product experience. Timing is everything here.
  • Retargeting trial users who didn't subscribe: Custom audiences of trial users who downloaded the app but didn't convert get retargeted with social proof — member count, class variety, testimonials — and limited-time pricing. The message isn't 'try Cult.fit'; they've already done that. The message is 'the window for this offer is closing'.
  • Flexible pricing removes commitment anxiety: Monthly, quarterly, and annual plans at different price points. The annual plan dramatically increases LTV and reduces churn risk; the monthly plan removes commitment anxiety for new subscribers. Offering both options converts more users overall than a single plan, even if it means some choose monthly over annual.
  • Cult Pass positioning removes the biggest objection: Traditional gym memberships lock you to one location. Cult Pass removes that objection entirely — subscribe once, attend anywhere, book any class. Every bottom-funnel ad that leads with 'access any center' is using the product architecture as the conversion argument.
  • Referral incentives at conversion moment: 'Bring a friend and get one month free' offers at the point of subscription decision — when the user is most enthusiastic and most likely to share. This converts referrals at the highest possible rate by timing the ask correctly.
Post-Conversion — Most Critical Stage
Retention & Renewal — The LTV Engine
+
  • Attendance frequency as the churn predictor: Members attending 3+ classes per week renew at dramatically higher rates than those attending once. Every retention mechanism — booking reminders, streak challenges, class recommendations — is designed to increase weekly attendance frequency in the critical first 30 days, when most churn happens.
  • Waitlist and scarcity mechanics: Popular classes have limited spots and waitlists. This creates urgency (book early or miss out), habit formation (plan your week around class slots), and perceived value (if it's hard to get into, it must be worth attending). The waitlist system is simultaneously a product feature and a retention marketing tool.
  • Class variety as an anti-churn mechanism: Yoga, HIIT, dance, boxing, strength training, Pilates, cycling — the variety prevents the boredom that kills gym memberships. A member who's explored 5 class types is far less likely to churn than one who's only ever attended HIIT. Cross-class recommendation nudges ('You loved HIIT — try our Strength class this Saturday') actively guide members toward variety.
  • Lapsed member win-back campaigns: Targeting expired subscribers on Meta and Google with personalized messaging: their specific class types, their nearest center, and a re-join offer tailored to why they lapsed. The 80% who churned because of scheduling conflicts get a message about the new 6am and 9pm classes. The ones who moved cities see the new center near their new location.
  • Cult.sport as a between-class touchpoint: D2C fitness equipment purchases keep the Cult brand in a member's daily life even on non-class days — the yoga mat, the resistance bands, the gym bag. This cross-sell strategy extends brand touchpoints and feeds member data back into the platform for better personalization.
04 — Creative Strategy

Make them feel like they're missing out.

Cult.fit's best creative doesn't describe fitness — it shows what a class actually feels like. Energy, community, and transformation are the message; the product format just delivers it.

🎬
Instagram Reels (15–30 sec)
High-energy class clips with music, trainer callouts, and real member reactions. The best-performing format because it shows the actual product experience in native format. The hook is always a feeling, not a feature — 10 seconds of a packed HIIT class converts better than 30 seconds of voiceover about class types and pricing.
📍
Hyper-Local Google Search Ads
'HIIT Classes in Indiranagar — Free First Class' with location extensions, call extensions, and site links to specific class schedules. The copy is locality-first, not brand-first. Someone searching for a gym in Koramangala doesn't care about Cult.fit's national presence — they care about the Koramangala center's class schedule and availability.
📲
Stories Ads with Direct CTA
Swipe-up to book a free trial. Countdown timers for limited-time pricing offers. Poll stickers asking 'Which class would you try first?' — engagement mechanics that simultaneously build audience signal and drive soft conversions. Stories' full-screen format creates immersive class experience previews that feed ads can't replicate.
⚡
YouTube Bumper Ads (6 sec)
A trainer's countdown, music pumping, class cheering — in 6 seconds. These non-skippable bumpers don't sell; they create a feeling. 6 seconds of class energy is just enough to plant FOMO before the main video the user came to watch. They work best as a top-of-funnel frequency-building tool alongside longer format awareness ads.
🏋️
Transformation & Community UGC
Member before-after stories, workout progress posts, class selfies, trainer shoutout compilations. Real people in real classes — not fitness models in a studio. The authenticity makes the aspiration feel achievable rather than unattainable. These also function as social proof for skeptical users in the consideration phase.
🗺️
Outdoor & Society Gate Branding
Large-format hoardings near centers and society gate branding in catchment areas. 'First Class Free — Book on the App' with a QR code. Local outdoor creates walk-in trial conversions that no digital channel can capture — the person who lives 800 meters from a center and walks past the hoarding every morning is a high-conversion prospect that Meta and Google will never find.
The creative insight that separates Cult.fit from every other fitness brand: They don't show fitness results — they show the experience of getting there. Most gym ads show transformation: before-after bodies, weight numbers, dress sizes. Cult.fit shows what Tuesday morning HIIT class feels like — the music, the trainer's energy, the members around you. The result they're selling isn't a body; it's a feeling you get three times a week. That's a fundamentally more compelling and sustainable creative strategy.

Why Their Creative Works

  • 01
    Shows the actual product, not a representation of it A 20-second class clip does more conversion work than any testimonial or claim because it answers the prospect's real question: 'What will I actually be doing?' The uncertainty about what a class feels like is the biggest barrier to trial. Remove the uncertainty, remove the barrier.
  • 02
    Trainer personalities create pre-class connection Cult.fit's trainers are positioned as personalities, not just instructors. Recurring trainer faces in ads create familiarity before the first class — you feel like you know them before you walk in. This familiarity dramatically increases the chance that a first trial leads to a repeat booking, which leads to subscription conversion.
  • 03
    Hyper-local messaging earns local trust An ad that names your specific neighborhood — Indiranagar, Koramangala, Bandra — signals that this isn't a generic national brand speaking to everyone. It's a local center speaking to you, specifically. This personalization, even when it's just geographic, meaningfully improves CTR and trial conversion rates in location-based businesses.
  • 04
    FOMO is structural, not manufactured Limited class spots and waitlists create urgency that the ads don't need to manufacture. 'Only 4 spots left in Saturday's boxing class' is a factually true statement that creates genuine urgency. The product design creates the scarcity; the creative just communicates it honestly. This is fundamentally more trustworthy than fake countdown timers.

What Doesn't Work As Well

  • App install ads often feel generic — 'Download and book your first class' doesn't differentiate Cult.fit from any fitness app; the creative needs to show WHY this specific app and what it unlocks
  • Pricing communication is confusing — multiple plans, dynamic pricing, and center-specific rates make the value proposition unclear in ads; simpler per-class framing would reduce decision paralysis
  • Lapsed user retargeting tends to be one-size-fits-all; users who churned because of relocation, scheduling conflicts, or budget constraints need different messages and offers
05 — Takeaways

What I took away from this.

Six strategic principles from Cult.fit's subscription marketing playbook — and four things I would push further to improve trial-to-subscription conversion and retention.

01
Local search is king for location-based businesses
GMB optimization and hyper-local Google Ads are more important than any Meta campaign. When someone searches 'gym near me' at 7am on a Monday, you need to be in the local pack top 3. That query has higher conversion intent than any awareness ad could generate. Miss it and no amount of Instagram Reels will compensate.
02
The trial IS the ad
For subscription services, the free first class experience sells better than any creative. Performance marketing's job is to get people through the door — the product does the rest. This means the quality of the first-class experience is as much a marketing problem as the quality of the Instagram creative. Both have to be right.
03
Retention > acquisition in unit economics
A 60% renewal rate and 90% NPS mean that every rupee spent on member experience and retention delivers more long-term value than the same rupee spent on acquisition. Most marketing teams are structured and incentivized to optimize acquisition; the better structural bet is to rebalance toward retention programs.
04
Habit-building is retention marketing
Streak tracking, class booking reminders, waitlists, and class variety aren't just product features — they're retention marketing tools that reduce churn without any additional media spend. The best retention programs are built into the product, not bolted on as email campaigns after the fact.
05
Subscription LTV changes the entire CAC math
Unlike e-commerce brands optimizing for single-purchase ROAS, Cult.fit can rationally afford a higher CAC because ₹500 trial → ₹16,000+ LTV. Performance marketers working in subscription businesses must anchor every bid decision to LTV, not immediate conversion value. A ₹1,200 CAC that generates ₹16,000 LTV is a better business than a ₹200 CAC that generates ₹600 LTV.
06
Community is an organic growth channel
A 90% recommendation rate means word-of-mouth is a genuine paid-channel equivalent at zero marginal cost. This isn't an accident — it's the result of a product experience that consistently delivers on its promise. When members become advocates, CAC drops and LTV extends through social proof effects on their networks. The best growth hack is building something worth recommending.
What I Would Optimize
  • 🎯
    Personalized Win-Back Campaigns for Lapsed Members
    80% of churned users want to come back — they just hit friction. The current retargeting likely treats all lapsed users the same. I would build dynamic audience segments based on churn reason: users who lapsed because of relocation see ads highlighting the new center in their area; users who lapsed during Ramadan get re-activation messaging in October; users whose most-attended class type is now available at expanded timings get that specific message. Personalized win-back based on behavioral history, not just 'We miss you' — the conversion rate difference would be significant.
  • 🤝
    Referral-Powered Performance Marketing
    With a 90% recommendation rate, referrals should be treated as a paid acquisition channel with its own budget, creative, and reporting. I would run Meta ads featuring real members doing 'bring a friend free' offers — not a CTA card but actual user video saying 'I've been coming for 6 months, bring a friend this week and both your first classes are free'. Track referral-driven CAC separately and compare to cold acquisition. The hypothesis: referred users have lower CAC, higher trial-to-subscription conversion, and better long-term retention — because they came in with social accountability already built in.
  • 📺
    YouTube Shorts as Primary Discovery Format
    15-second class experience clips on YouTube Shorts would reach fitness-curious users who haven't yet searched for a gym — people watching fitness content, workout videos, or health content organically. This is largely untapped top-of-funnel reach that bridges the gap between the high-intent Google Search user and the passive Instagram Reel viewer. Shorts' native discovery algorithm also means content with strong watch-through can earn organic reach on top of paid distribution — a content efficiency Reels can also deliver but Shorts specifically reaches a different, slightly older demographic on YouTube.
  • 💰
    Per-Class Cost Framing in All Bottom-Funnel Ads
    Showing '₹1,999/month' makes the ask feel large in bottom-funnel ads. Showing '₹150/class for unlimited classes' makes the same price feel like a bargain — especially compared to a single PT session at ₹1,000+. I would A/B test per-class framing vs monthly pricing across all conversion-stage ads. The hypothesis is that per-class cost framing increases subscription conversion rate by making the value proposition immediately comparable to alternatives the user is already familiar with. This single copy change, if validated, would have significant impact on conversion rates at no additional media cost.
