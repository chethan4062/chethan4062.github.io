01 — Brand Overview

The Ordinary, built for India.

Minimalist didn't invent actives-led skincare — they made it accessible, transparent, and locally relevant. That's a harder problem than it sounds.

Founded
2020 — Jaipur, Rajasthan
Founders
Mohit Yadav & Rahul Yadav
Category
Premium actives-led skincare & haircare (D2C)
Mission
#HideNothing — radical ingredient transparency
Acquisition
Acquired by HUL in January 2025 at ₹2,955 Cr (~$350M) — one of the largest D2C exits in India
Investors
Peak XV Partners (Sequoia India) — Series A
Positioning
Inspired by The Ordinary (Canada) but engineered for Indian skin types, climate, and price sensitivity. Products named after their active ingredients — not brand fantasy names — which created instant consumer trust and powerful SEO signals simultaneously.

What makes this extraordinary: Minimalist was profitable from inception — a rarity in Indian D2C where most brands burn cash chasing GMV. Their ~65% gross margins meant they could reinvest 40–50% of revenue into marketing without ever being loss-led. The growth wasn't funded by discounting; it was funded by genuine product-market fit.

The brand's masterstroke was the naming convention. By calling products '10% Niacinamide + Zinc' instead of 'Glow Serum' or 'Blemish Fix', they did three things at once: built immediate trust with skincare-educated consumers, created a natural SEO moat (ingredient queries = buying intent), and forced competitors into a comparison they couldn't win without matching the transparency.

The #HideNothing mission wasn't just a tagline — it was a complete creative and communication framework. Every ad, every packaging decision, every landing page echoed the same philosophy. This brand coherence is exceptionally rare and is a large part of why the performance marketing worked so well: the message was consistent from cold ad to checkout page to post-purchase email.

03 — Funnel Breakdown

From cold scroll to loyal customer.

Minimalist's funnel is elegant because it mirrors how skincare decisions actually happen: education first, trust second, purchase third, and a repeatable retention loop after. Click each stage to expand.

Top of Funnel
Awareness — Educate & Attract
+
  • Instagram Reels & Carousels: Ingredient education as the hook — 'What does 10% Niacinamide actually do to your skin?' Teaches first, sells second. These perform best because they stop the scroll without feeling like ads.
  • YouTube Pre-roll: Dermatologist-style explainers for 'hyaluronic acid for dry skin', 'retinol for beginners' — capturing high-intent users researching before they buy.
  • Micro-influencer seeding: Skincare creators (50K–500K), not Bollywood celebrities. Authentic, ingredient-accurate reviews that align with the #HideNothing positioning. ROAS on seeded content outperforms paid celebrity deals in this category.
  • SEO Content Flywheel: DA 60+, 64,000+ backlinks, ranking for 'best serum for acne scars', 'niacinamide benefits', 'SPF 50 sunscreen India'. Organic traffic reduces paid acquisition dependency over time — this is the structural advantage most D2C brands miss.
Mid Funnel
Consideration — Build Trust & Reduce Friction
+
  • Retargeting with product-specific creative: If you viewed the Niacinamide 10% serum, you're retargeted with that exact SKU — not generic brand ads. Dynamic Product Ads on Meta handle this at scale.
  • UGC & review-based ads: Before-after skin transformation content from real customers. These convert significantly better than polished brand creative in skincare because the result is the product demo.
  • Ingredient Spotlight content: 'Why SPF 50 vs SPF 30 actually matters' — deepens trust and moves users closer to purchase without a hard sell.
  • Comparison content: 'Minimalist vs The Ordinary' is a search term people type. Minimalist ranked for it, capturing users who are already considering buying actives — just deciding which brand.
  • Email nurture: Website visitors who didn't purchase receive educational sequences — skin type guides, ingredient 101s — that prime them for conversion on the next visit.
Bottom of Funnel
Conversion — Close the Sale
+
  • Dynamic Product Ads (Meta): Price, rating, and product image in a single unit for cart abandoners. Urgency without gimmicks — the product's price point does the heavy lifting.
  • Google Shopping: Competitive pricing is visible before the click — ₹599 for a serum vs ₹1,800+ for imports. This is a conversion argument built into the ad format itself.
  • Amazon Sponsored Products: 'Buy niacinamide serum India' is high commercial intent. Minimalist shows up here with ratings, Prime badge, and competitive price. Conversion rates on marketplace are structurally higher than DTC site.
  • Cart abandonment retargeting: Meta + email within 1–3 hours of abandonment. First-time buyer offers (free shipping, introductory bundle) close the hesitant segment.
  • Bundle deals at checkout: Routine-building cross-sells — 'Add the Vitamin C serum to complete your morning routine' — increase AOV at the point of highest intent.
Post-Purchase
Retention — The LTV Engine
+
  • Replenishment email triggers: Serums last ~45 days. An automated email at day 35 ('Your Niacinamide is running low') with a one-click reorder link converts at significantly higher rates than cold acquisition — and at near-zero marginal cost.
  • Cross-sell campaigns: Bought a serum? The next recommended product is sunscreen (non-negotiable for actives users), then moisturizer. Systematically building the 3-step routine increases LTV 3–4× per customer.
  • UGC flywheel: Post-purchase emails requesting reviews and social shares. This UGC then feeds back into the TOFU ad creative — a self-reinforcing content loop that reduces creative production costs.
  • WhatsApp Commerce: Reorder convenience for repeat customers. Low-friction channel for replenishment nudges in a market where WhatsApp penetration is near-universal.
  • Education as retention: 'Your 4-week skin progress check-in', 'how to layer your actives correctly' — content that makes customers feel invested in the routine, not just the product.
04 — Creative Strategy

The ad is the education.

Minimalist's creative approach broke every conventional D2C playbook rule — and it worked precisely because it did.

📋
Instagram Carousels
Highest engagement format in their mix. '5 things you didn't know about Hyaluronic Acid', 'Niacinamide vs Salicylic Acid — which one do you need?' Each slide is a micro-lesson. Users save and share these, giving organic reach on paid spend.
🎬
Reels / Short Video
Quick skincare routines, ingredient explainers, before-after transformations in 30–60 seconds. Hook in the first 2 seconds is always a problem statement: 'If you have oily skin and still get dehydrated…'. Stops the scroll by speaking directly to the viewer's pain.
📸
Static Product Creative
Clinical, white-background product photography with ingredient percentage callouts. Deliberately anti-glamorous. The pack itself is the hero — minimal design that signals scientific credibility rather than aspirational luxury. Works especially well in Google Shopping.
👥
UGC Compilation Ads
Real customer before-after results, raw phone footage, honest reviews. These outperform polished brand creative on conversion because the result IS the product demo. Authenticity signals in skincare carry significantly more weight than production quality.
📺
YouTube Mid-Roll (60–90 seconds)
Educational content that feels indistinguishable from organic YouTube videos. Dermatologist-style delivery, clinical framing, no hard sell until the final 10 seconds. These ads build brand equity alongside driving conversions — something most performance-only creatives fail to do. The dual purpose (brand + direct response) makes the channel exceptionally capital-efficient.
The insight that drives everything: Minimalist's target customer is already educated. They know what niacinamide does. They've read the Reddit threads. They watch skincare YouTube. Treating them as intelligent — showing the exact % of active ingredients, explaining the science without dumbing it down — is itself a differentiator in a category full of vague promises.

Why Their Creative Works

  • 01
    Consistency from cold ad to checkout The brand voice, visual identity, and messaging are identical across every touchpoint — Instagram ad, Nykaa listing, product packaging, email. This coherence builds trust faster than any single creative element can.
  • 02
    High creative refresh rate Each new product launch (42+ SKUs) is also a content event — an ingredient spotlight that generates fresh creative organically. The product roadmap and the creative calendar are the same calendar. This structural advantage keeps fatigue low without needing a large creative team.
  • 03
    Social proof baked into the creative unit '4.3★ from 8,400 reviews', 'Amazon's Choice', '#1 Bestseller in Serums' — these aren't just trust badges on landing pages. They're embedded directly into ad creative, compressing the consideration phase before the click happens.
  • 04
    Anti-celebrity, pro-credibility casting No Bollywood endorsements. Dermatologists, skincare chemists, and everyday users with visible skin improvements. In a post-influencer-fatigue market, this casting choice signals that the product works on scientific merit — not because a celebrity was paid to say so.
05 — Takeaways

What I took away from this.

Six strategic principles from Minimalist's growth playbook — and four things I would push further if I were running their performance marketing.

01
Education IS the ad
Minimalist proved you don't need celebrity endorsements when your content genuinely solves a problem. Information-dense creative that respects the audience's intelligence outperforms hype in high-consideration categories.
02
Naming as a growth lever
Calling products '10% Niacinamide + Zinc' instead of 'Glow Serum' created instant search intent, built SEO moats, and forced competitors into a transparency arms race. The product name was simultaneously positioning, SEO, and performance marketing strategy.
03
Price-value discipline
Competing with The Ordinary at similar or lower price points, while being locally stocked and Amazon Prime-eligible, removed every purchase barrier for the Indian consumer. Price + availability + trust = conversion without discounting.
04
D2C + Marketplace hybrid
Not putting all eggs in one basket. Own site for brand building and higher margins, Amazon for volume and trust (ratings + Prime), Nykaa for beauty-intent discovery. Each channel serves a distinct role in the growth engine.
05
Profitable from day one
Disciplined CAC management and ~65% gross margins let them reinvest 40–50% of revenue into marketing — without VC pressure to grow at any cost. Profitable growth gives you strategic patience that loss-making brands never have.
06
Content-led SEO flywheel
Educational blog content (DA 60+, 64K+ backlinks) drove organic traffic that continuously reduced their dependency on paid acquisition. Most brands treat SEO and paid as separate budgets. Minimalist treated them as a unified customer acquisition system.
What I Would Optimize
  • 💬
    Deeper WhatsApp Commerce Integration
    Skincare replenishment cycles (30–45 days) are structurally perfect for WhatsApp reorder flows. An automated nudge at day 25 with a single-tap reorder link would capture the highest-intent repeat purchase moment — before the customer runs out and opens Amazon instead. In a market with near-universal WhatsApp penetration, this is an underexploited LTV lever.
  • 🗣️
    Vernacular Content Expansion
    Minimalist's education-first approach is currently English-heavy — which caps its reach at Tier 1 urban audiences. Hindi and regional language Reels and ads could unlock the Tier 2/3 consumer who is equally interested in skincare but scrolls in their native language. The product formulations already work for Indian skin. The content language should follow.
  • 🤖
    AI-Powered Personalization at Scale
    A skincare quiz (skin type, concerns, current routine) → personalized product bundle recommendation → dynamic bundle ads across Meta and Google retargeting that show the exact recommended routine. This reduces decision paralysis for new users (42 SKUs is overwhelming) and increases first-order AOV by showing a curated 3-step stack rather than a single product.
  • 🔁
    Subscription Model for Core SKUs
    Serums and sunscreens have predictable 30–45 day usage cycles. A subscribe-and-save model (10–15% discount) would lock in LTV, smooth revenue predictability, and dramatically reduce re-acquisition costs. The customer who subscribes has already made a commitment to the routine — the hardest part of skincare CAC is already done. Post-HUL acquisition, this is also a natural fit for HUL's existing D2C infrastructure.
